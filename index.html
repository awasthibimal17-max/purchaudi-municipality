Municipality Overview

A Municipality
Born from Ten Villages

Purchaudi Municipality is a local government unit in Baitadi District of Sudurpashchim Province, Nepal. It was formally established on 10 March 2017 when the Government of Nepal restructured the country's administrative units under the new Constitution of 2015, merging ten historic village development committees into one cohesive municipality.

The municipality's headquarters sits at Hat town, a hilltop market settlement that has served as the commercial and civic heart of this corner of the far-western hills for generations. The municipality spans 198.52 square kilometres of rugged, forested terrain, with elevation varying from gentle river valleys to high ridgelines typical of the Sudurpashchim hills.

With a population of 39,174 spread across 10 wards and 6,390 households, Purchaudi is a community deeply rooted in agricultural tradition, Khas-Arya culture, and the ancient rhythms of hill life — while gradually embracing the connectivity and governance reforms that Nepal's federal era is bringing to remote communities.

People & Society

Community,
Language & Caste

Purchaudi Municipality is home to a population that is deeply embedded in the Khas-Arya traditions of far-western Nepal. The Chhetri caste forms the overwhelming majority, followed by Brahmin-Hill, Dalit communities (including Kami, Sarki, Damai/Dholi, and Lohar), and smaller groups of Thakuri and others.

The municipality had 19,023 males and 20,151 females per the 2011 census, giving a gender ratio of 0.94 — reflecting the out-migration of young men to India and Gulf countries in search of economic opportunity, a pattern common across Sudurpashchim Province.

With 88 schools serving around 9,465 students (as of 2017 records), and an average of 108 students per school, education access in Purchaudi is growing — though rural communities still face challenges with quality schooling and dropout rates among girls.

Living Culture

Faith, Festival
& Folk Tradition

Gaura Parva — Heart of Sudurpashchim

Gaura Parva is the defining festival of Purchaudi and all of Sudurpashchim Province — so deeply embedded in local identity that it carries greater emotional weight than even Dashain for many families. The festival commemorates the divine wedding of Goddess Gaura (Parvati) to Lord Shiva, and falls during the Nepali month of Bhadra (August–September).

It is led by women, who observe fasting, sing sacred Saguna hymns, perform the communal Deuda circular dance, and wear the sacred duvadhago thread — a female counterpart to the male bratabandha ritual. The festival is a public holiday in Sudurpashchim Province, and many people abroad return home especially for the occasion.

Day 1
Biruda Panchami Women soak five sacred grains — wheat, horse gram, pea, rhododendron seed, and black gram (Pancha Biruda) — in a copper vessel purified with cow dung and decorated with dubo grass.
Day 2
Shashthi Ritual washing of soaked grains; women fast and observe celibacy. Deuda songs fill the community grounds as inter-village gatherings begin.
Day 3
Saptami The Pancha Biruda is carried to the temple in a procession. Fasting women (Bartalu/Upavasi) sing hymns continuously.
Day 4
Gaurashtami The Goddess Gaura effigy is created and taken to the temple courtyard (Gaura Khala). Biruda is distributed as prasad — blessings for long life and family prosperity. Idol immersed in river 5–11 days later.

Deuda Dance

The Deuda is Sudurpashchim's signature folk dance — performers form circles, linking arms while singing improvised songs about love, hardship, and community. Women lead the singing at Gaura, conveying social commentary across generations. Styles vary between Chaitali, Dhumari, and Dhusko.

Folk Tradition

Dashain & Tihar

As in all of Nepal, Dashain and Tihar are celebrated with great devotion — buffalo and goat sacrifices, the goddess Durga's blessings, and rows of oil lamps during Tihar. The Hat market sees a surge in trade as families prepare.

Hindu Festival

Baitadeli Language

Virtually the entire municipality — 38,422 of 39,174 people — speaks Baitadeli as their first language. This regional dialect, related to Doteli, carries centuries of oral tradition, folk songs, and storytelling that define the community's identity.

Linguistic Heritage

Chhaliya Dance & Oral Traditions

Beyond Deuda, the Chhaliya sword dance and lok geet (folk songs) form part of social gatherings. These art forms preserve historical memory and are performed during weddings, harvests, and community celebrations across Purchaudi's wards.

Economy & Livelihood

Agriculture,
Trade & Migration

🌽

Staple Crops

Maize and wheat are the primary crops. Millet, barley, and rice are grown for household consumption on the terraced hillside fields that characterise Baitadi's agricultural landscape.

Hat Town — Weekly Market Hub

Hat town, the municipality's headquarters, takes its very name from the Nepali word for a periodic open market (haat). The traditional weekly market brings together farmers, artisans, and traders from all ten wards and surrounding villages. Smallholders sell seasonal produce directly to buyers — maize, vegetables, fruits, and medicinal herbs — bypassing middlemen. The Hat market is not merely economic infrastructure; it is the social centre where community news spreads, disputes are resolved, and bonds between villages are affirmed.

🍊

Fruits & Horticulture

Mandarins, oranges, lemons, and apples are grown commercially across Baitadi's hills. Fresh fruit from Purchaudi's farms is exported to Dhangadhi and Bhimdatta. Emblica (amla) also grows both cultivated and wild.

🌿

Medicinal Herbs

The forests of Sudurpashchim yield valuable medicinal and aromatic plants. These herbs form part of local trade at the Hat market and hold potential for higher-value agro-processing.

✈️

Labour Migration

A significant portion of working-age males migrate seasonally to India and Gulf countries. Male absentees numbered 862 in 2011 — nearly 4.5% of all males — reflecting a broad economic pattern across far-western Nepal.

⚡

Infrastructure Growth

Small-scale hydropower and solar energy projects are extending electricity access to Purchaudi's rural wards. Road connectivity via the Mahakali Highway feeder roads is gradually improving, though monsoon landslides remain a challenge.

Historical Background

Deep Roots
in the Khas Kingdom

The land that is now Purchaudi Municipality shares the ancient heritage of Baitadi District — one of three major centres of the Khas kingdom, a region that has seen Katyuri kings, the Khas dynasty, Chand rulers, and eventually the unification of Nepal under Prithvi Narayan Shah. The municipality's creation in 2017 is only the latest chapter in a very long story.

~10th C.

Khas Kingdom — One of Three Great Centres

After the fall of the Great Katyuri kingdom, Baitadi — the land encompassing present-day Purchaudi — was seized by Khas King Ashok Challa of Sapadalaksh. Baitadi became one of three major centres of the Khas kingdom, alongside Kamadesh (Kali Kumaun) and Kedarbhumi (Garhwal). This history gives Purchaudi's people a direct link to the foundations of Khas-Nepali culture and language.

Medieval Era

The Chand Dynasty

According to historical folk-tales, a Chand king fought the Khas ruler and established a sovereign Chand dynasty state in Baitadi. Historians link this to the broader establishment of the Kumaun Kingdom. The Chand era saw Baitadi develop its own distinct cultural and administrative identity, with the Gwallek Kedar sacred forest — one of four Kedars along the Indo-Nepal border — as a key spiritual landmark.

18th–19th C.

Unification & the Far-Western Hills

Baitadi was incorporated into the unified Kingdom of Nepal. The far-western hills maintained their distinct Baitadeli-speaking culture and periodic haat market tradition. The village of Hat, future headquarters of Purchaudi, served as a local market hub for surrounding communities.

1962

Baitadi Recognised as Distinct District

Nepal's administrative reorganisation formalised Baitadi as one of 75 (now 77) districts, covering 1,519 km². This gave formal recognition to the ten communities — Kotila, Bhatana, Bhumiraj, Kuwakot, Nwadeu, Mahadevsthan, Hat, Malladehi, Talladehi, and Bijayapur — that would later merge into Purchaudi Municipality.

2015

New Constitution of Nepal

Nepal promulgated a new federal constitution, reorganising the country into seven provinces and 753 local government units. This laid the groundwork for the creation of Purchaudi Municipality as a distinct administrative entity with its own elected government.

10 Mar 2017

Purchaudi Municipality Established ★

The Government of Nepal formally announced 744 local level units. Ten village development committees — Kotila, Bhatana, Bhumiraj, Kuwakot, Nwadeu, Mahadevsthan, Hat, Malladehi, Talladehi, and Bijayapur — were merged to create Purchaudi Municipality. Hat town was designated as headquarters. The municipality became operational in May 2017.

Getting There

How to Reach
Purchaudi Municipality

✈️

Via Air — Dhangadhi Airport

1
Fly Kathmandu → Dhangadhi Airport (DHI) in Kailali, Sudurpashchim Province. Multiple daily flights, ~1 hour.
2
Take a local bus or hire a jeep via the Mahakali Highway towards Dasharathchand (Baitadi HQ) — approx. 4–5 hours.
3
From Dasharathchand, continue on the feeder road to Hat town, Purchaudi — approximately 30–45 minutes by vehicle.
🚌

Via Long-Distance Bus

1
Board a night bus from Kathmandu (New Bus Park) towards Mahendranagar or Dhangadhi — approx. 18–22 hours journey.
2
Alight at Jhulaghat or Baitadi junction on the Mahakali Highway, then take a local vehicle heading uphill to Dasharathchand.
3
From Dasharathchand (Baitadi HQ), take a local jeep or shared vehicle to Hat town, the seat of Purchaudi Municipality.
🛣️

Via Patan Airport (Baitadi)

1
Baitadi has its own domestic airstrip at Patan — check current flight availability from Kathmandu (Nepali Airlines / private carriers).
2
From Patan, hire transport to Dasharathchand and then onward to Hat town. This is the fastest route when flights are operational.
